\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

\paragraph{Baseline divergence (cell\_000).} The OT path denominator $1-(1-\sigma_{\min})t$ reaches $10^{-4}$ near $t{=}1$, producing velocity magnitudes ${\sim}10^4$. Under fp16, this overflows after ${\sim}18{,}700$ steps. BatchOT prevents this: by minimizing transport cost, it keeps $\|x_1 - x_0\|$ small, so both the numerator and denominator of Eq.~\ref{eq:ot_vf} remain moderate. BatchOT is not just a variance reducer---it is a \emph{numerical stabilizer} for the OT path.

\paragraph{StableVM collapse (cells 010/011/110/111).} The log-probability (Eq.~\ref{eq:log_prob}) contains the term $-d\log\sigma_t$. For CIFAR-10 ($d = 3{\times}32{\times}32 = 3072$) near $t{=}1$:
\begin{equation*}
    -d\log\sigma_t \approx -3072 \times \log(10^{-4}) \approx 28{,}300.
\end{equation*}
This enormous value causes the softmax over importance weights to collapse onto a single reference sample, producing a noisy, numerically unstable target. In 2D ($d{=}2$), the same term yields only ${\sim}18.4$, explaining why StableVM works perfectly in Study~1 but oscillates wildly on CIFAR-10.

\paragraph{TPC gradient explosion (cells 001/101).} The consistency penalty $\|\vt(\xt,t_1) - \vt(\xt,t_2)\|^2$ compares velocities at $t_1 \approx 0$ and $t_2 \approx 1$. Near $t{=}1$, velocity magnitudes are ${\sim}1/\sigma_{\min} = 10^4$; near $t{=}0$, they are $O(1)$. The penalty gradient is dominated by the large-$t$ term, causing gradient explosions that trigger NaN at steps 14--17k.

\subsection{Answers to Research Questions}

\textbf{RQ1:} The three axes are \emph{not} additive---combining BatchOT with StableVM or TPC \emph{destroys} the convergence that BatchOT alone provides. \textbf{RQ2:} BatchOT is unambiguously the most impactful single axis, consistent with \citet{tong2024improving}. \textbf{RQ3:} Geometric insights transfer (OT paths are straighter, solvers are more efficient), but stability insights do not: techniques safe in 2D fail catastrophically at $d{=}3072$.

\subsection{Limitations}

All cells used a single seed, fast mode (9M parameters, 80k steps), and fp16 mixed precision without gradient clipping. Gradient clipping and fp32 training might rescue the baseline (cell\_000), but the StableVM and TPC instabilities are structural---they stem from dimension-dependent scaling, not precision. The two studies use different codebases (MLP vs.\ U-Net), so direct numerical comparisons between them are not meaningful.

\subsection{Conclusion}

BatchOT coupling is validated as the key variance-reduction ingredient for CFM. StableVM and TPC require dimension-aware redesigns before deployment on image data: normalizing importance weights by $1/d$, restricting TPC pairs to $t \in [0.1, 0.9]$, or detaching the consistency penalty from the gradient. More broadly, variance-reduction techniques must be validated not only for their statistical properties but for their numerical stability in the target dimension.
